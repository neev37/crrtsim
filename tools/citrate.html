How this works

The idea. Citrate chelates ionized calcium in the circuit, so coagulation can't proceed in the filter. Post-filter calcium is given back into the venous return to restore systemic ionized Ca.

Citrate infusion rate.

Rate (mL/h) = (Dose × Qb × 60) / [citrate]

Qb in mL/min, dose in mmol/L blood, concentration in mmol/L. Typical adult dose is 3 mmol/L blood flow.

Calcium replacement. A fraction of total Ca (~60%) is bound to citrate and lost in the effluent. The starting Ca infusion estimates this loss:

Ca loss (mmol/h) ≈ 0.6 × Qeffluent × [Ca]total

Then convert to mL/h based on whichever Ca solution you're using. Always titrate to systemic iCa.

Bicarbonate. Each citrate molecule, when metabolized (mostly hepatic), yields 3 bicarbonate equivalents. About half the infused citrate is removed in effluent before reaching systemic circulation, so the simulator estimates the net patient bicarb load as citrate × 1.5. Useful when choosing a lower-bicarb dialysate to prevent alkalosis.

Watch out for accumulation. Liver failure or hypoperfusion → citrate accumulates → Ca-citrate complex builds → measured total Ca rises, iCa falls. Total Ca / iCa > 2.5 is the classic flag.
