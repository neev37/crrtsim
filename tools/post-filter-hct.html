How this works

Red cells don't cross the membrane — so as plasma is filtered off, the same red cell mass occupies a smaller volume. The math is conservation of red cell flux:

Hctpost = (Qb × Hctpre) / (Qb − Qpost − Qnet UF)

Pre-dilution dilutes the blood before the filter, lowering the effective Hct entering the membrane and giving you headroom:

Hctinlet = Qb × Hctpre / (Qb + Qpre)

Targets. Keep post-filter Hct below ~40% if you can; 40–50% is workable but filter life shortens; above 50% expect frequent clotting. The single most powerful lever is pre-dilution.

Why dialysate isn't here. Diffusive solute movement doesn't shift water across the membrane in net, so dialysate flow doesn't change post-filter Hct directly — but it still drives delivered dose. See the FF tool for the full picture.

Evidence base

Filter life vs hemoconcentration: Joannidis M, Oudemans-van Straaten HM. Clinical review: patency of the circuit in continuous renal replacement therapy. Crit Care. 2007;11:218.

Pre-dilution doubles filter life: van der Voort PHJ et al. Int J Artif Organs. 2005;28(4):371–377.

Hct as a clotting predictor: Tan HK et al. Factors related to the haemodynamic and biochemical effects of CVVH. Crit Care Med. 2002;30:1518–1523.
